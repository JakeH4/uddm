Welcome

I play a lot of GeoGuessr. It drops you into a random Google Street View somewhere in the world, and you have to figure out where you are and drop a pin on the map. The closer your pin, the more points you score, up to 5000 a round.

I've put enough hours into it that I'm decent now. I can usually name the country in the first few seconds, and on a lot of rounds I get the region or even the road. A big part of getting better was studying PlonkIt, which has country-by-country guides that cover the things that give a location away.

What I actually look at

  • Driving side. The left side cuts the world down to a shorter list of countries.
  • Road lines and bollards. The center line colors and the posts along the road are specific to each country.
  • The Google car. Camera generation, car color, and the antenna can point you to one country.
  • License plates and signs. Plate shapes and colors, plus any text, usually confirm it.
  • Landscape and sun. Vegetation and soil narrow the region, and the sun's position helps with the hemisphere.

Getting better

What moved me from guessing countries to placing pins close was learning the meta from PlonkIt and playing a lot of rounds, mostly duels. By now, when I drop in, I'm not really guessing, I'm reading the clues. The rounds that still get me are the empty ones with no roads, signs, or cars, where you go off the vegetation and a gut feeling.

The niche stuff

The deeper you go, the more it comes down to small regional details. A lot of them are poles and roadside markers. A few I can name on sight:

Power poles

  • Stobie poles, the concrete and steel power poles you mostly see in South Australia.
  • Octagonal concrete poles, a giveaway for Mexico.
  • The diagonal yellow and black striped poles in Taiwan.

Roadside markers

  • Ecuadorian crosses on the side of the road.
